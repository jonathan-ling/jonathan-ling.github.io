Prioritizing Order Scheduling Under Resource Constraints in a CPG Supply Chain

Niagara Bottling internship

Operations Research Mathematical Optimization

In supply chain distribution, customer orders are fulfillable only if all the product and logistical resources required to do so are available; otherwise, it must be rescheduled. Three key resources required are the product, carrier (i.e., truck) and dock (i.e., warehouse loading space). To avoid wasting scheduling efforts, schedulers must decide whether to schedule or reschedule each order with respect to the customersâ€™ desired delivery date before taking that action, while considering penalties to reschedule. In this project, I formulated a binary integer program as a variant of the knapsack problem in operations research, to optimize the prioritization sequence of orders under resource contention constraints.

Developing a Digital Scheduling Product for the US Air Force

Stanford University course project (MS&E 297 - "Hacking for Defense": Solving National Security issues with the Lean Launchpad) taught by Steve Blank, pioneer of the Lean Launchpad methodology

Entrepreneurship Lean Launchpad Customer Discovery Operations Research

Within the US Air Force, mobility squadron schedulers resort to manual schedule generation methods, including using whiteboards and Excel spreadsheets. This often leads to brittle schedules, with which enacting last-minute changes results in significant ramifications on squadron morale while impacting personal and training time. During this course, our team conducted 130+ customer discovery interivews and iterated through several MVPs (minimum viable products) to develop a digital scheduling product that would improve the disparate interfaces schedulers use while optimizing schedule generation algorithmically, to lead to improved scheduling efficiency, crew rest and safety, and deployment responsiveness.

Dynamically Pricing Airline Tickets Using Reinforcement Learning

Stanford University course project (AA 228 - Decision Making Under Uncertainty)

Data Science Machine Learning

In the airline industry, dynamic pricing is a common tool for revenue management, where an airline's goal is to set ticket prices to maximize its revenue over a finite time horizon. This paper considers the case of pricing a specific inventory item (e.g., economy class tickets) that is sold to different customer segments based on their inputs when purchasing the ticket (e.g., days to departure, one way/return, length of trip, etc.) and which thus has different price points. We review common approaches taken to solve the single-segment (i.e., single price point) dynamic pricing problem and formulate a multi-segment (i.e., multiple price points) dynamic pricing problem using a factored multi-agent Markov decision process (MMDP) framework. We solve the multi-segment dynamic pricing problem using modern reinforcement learning (RL) approaches including Sarsa and Sarsa-Lambda, and compare the performance of these approaches to various baseline policies.

Predicting Repeat Callers with Classifier Models

Accenture project with an energy utlity client

Data Science Machine Learning

This project aimed to help an energy utility client streamline its customer contact points and reduce call center costs. In terms of analytics, I was involved in designing a database for the purpose of multi-channel analysis (that is, across the various channels that customers would contact the client, such as by phone, website and app) and then used this to develop a predictive model to classify repeat callers to proactively manage their main call reasons. For the first task, I designed the multi-channel database as an analytic record, an architectural pattern that has the advantage of being easily added to by a real-time stream of records, and which is readily consumable by a machine learning model. I also created random forest and XGBoost classifier models in Python to predict repeat callers, and used 'average precision' as the performance metric, as it works better than the standard 'accuracy' metric when the classes (in this case, repeat callers and non-repeat callers) are highly imbalanced. The random forest model achieved an average precision score of 0.7, translating into potential savings in proactive call management of several million dollars per year.

Predicting Modem Flaps with Decision Tree Classification

Accenture project with a telco client

Data Science Machine Learning Data Visualization

This project aimed to help a telco client improve its ability to manage modem flaps (intermittent connectivity dropouts from the network). Flapping issues could be classified as modem-based (originating from a specific customer's modem) or network-based (a network-wide problem). These two cases should be treated differently to be cost-efficient, but the client did not have the ability to distinguish them. To do so, I built a classifier model by using a decision tree-based model on several feature variables, as well as a graph database (Neo4j) to identify close-proximity groups of problematic nodes. To aid modeling and stakeholder communication, I also visualized the telco network graph, both geographically (with R's leaflet package) and logically (with R's visNetwork package).

Improving Workforce Management Processes with the Theory of Constraints

Accenture project with a telco client

Operations Analytics Theory of Constraints

This project aimed to help a telco client improve the forecasting accuracy and capacity management processes of their technician workforce. With their original operational model, new customers would schedule an appointment with the client to a get a technician to install a connection from their home to the network. However, this demand was difficult to forecast, being highly volatile from day to day, which made it expensive for the client as they had to continuously expand and contract their workforce. From the outset of the project, I challenged the idea that improving forecasting accuracy was the real goal. Using the Theory of Constraints, a management paradigm built on operations research principles, I identified the overarching goal (connecting end users efficiently to the network) and key conflict (lead time required) to propose a solution that would not only improve the project's initial KPIs but also significantly reduce operational expenses. Though not implemented due to scope constraints, it gained endorsement from the client.

The goal was to find a layout that minimized the cost of the cabling and new infrastructure along streets, while still connecting all homes. Mathematically, we formulated this as a mixed integer program and solved it using Gurobi. My contribution to this project was in designing and implementing rules to avoided cables trespassing on private property. For a zipcode of 20,000 premises, this was a mixed integer program with 200,000 variables and 200,000 constraints.
